Modes of Transportation

Truck, rail, air, ocean or pipeline? The right answer depends entirely on the shipment. This tool scores all five against the cargo you describe.

1

Project overview

Every mode of transportation trades one thing for another — speed for cost, flexibility for capacity. Chapter 4 lays out five modes and the questions that decide between them: what is the product, how urgent is it, and what restrictions does it carry? Moving wheat to Australia is nothing like shipping live lobster across the country.

  • What inspired it: the chapter's "shipping challenge" — given a product, work out the best way to move it — and the summary chart contrasting all five modes.
  • The concept: there is no single best mode, only the best fit. Weight, distance, urgency, value, perishability and product form push the decision toward different modes.
  • Why it matters: mode choice sets both the cost and the speed of the entire supply chain — and a wrong call (air-freighting bulk gravel, shipping vaccines by sea) is expensive or impossible.

    What did I learn?

    Knowledge // concepts

    • The five modes and the distinct niche each one owns.
    • Shipping categories — parcel, LTL, FTL, LCL, FCL and bulk.
    • Why intermodal shipping and containers changed global trade.

    Skills // abilities

    • Multi-criteria decision modelling — weighting several factors into one score.
    • Encoding hard constraints (pipelines can't carry boxes; trucks can't cross oceans).
    • Visualising a trade-off space rather than a single number.

    Reflection // real world

    • Surprising: ~80% of the world's goods move by sea — the slow mode dominates.
    • Difficult: tuning the weights so urgency and value pull air up without breaking ties.
    • Applied: this is the first question a freight forwarder asks about any new lane.
