The Role of Logistics
in Supply Chains

Logistics is the slice of a supply chain that moves and stores materials. Before anything else, you have to see the whole network — and the four things flowing through it.

1

Project overview

A supply chain is the whole network that creates a product and delivers it to a customer — sourcing, manufacturing, sales, finance and more. Logistics is the subset that physically moves and stores things along that network. This first project sets the foundation for every project that follows: you can't price a freight move, choose a mode, or design a warehouse until you can picture where it sits in the chain.

  • What inspired it: Chapter 1's diagrams of upstream/downstream flow and the opening idea that the coffee on your desk arrived through a product supply chain.
  • The concept: goods, information, money and returns move through tiered supply chains — and logistics, split into internal (inside one site) and external (between sites), connects every step.
  • Why it matters: the goal of logistics is delivery to the right place, at the right time, at the right price. You can only hit that target if you understand the network it travels through.

Each stage in the chain has its own logistics job — receiving, internal movement, storage and shipping. Upstream tiers (left) feed downstream tiers (right); the end customer is where demand starts and where reverse flows begin.

Internal vs external logistics: internal logistics moves parts and products within a single facility (forklifts on the plant floor); external logistics moves them between locations — suppliers, factories, distribution centres and customers. Almost every box above involves both.
3

Evidence

Chapter 1 distinguishes several kinds of supply chain. Each carries different "cargo," but all of them depend on logistics to move and store it. Below is my recreated summary, plus the chapter's bottled-water cost prompt that made the role of logistics concrete.

Product

Physical goods flow from raw materials through manufacturing to the end user. The classic forward chain.

Information

Data and documents (orders, bills of lading, status) move through systems and EDI, then get stored in databases.

Service

Intangible outputs (a haircut, legal advice) still need products, knowledge and talent supplied behind them.

Reverse

Returned, damaged or end-of-life products flow backward — downstream to upstream — for refund, repair or scrap.

Circular

Old products and waste are fed back as raw materials for a new cycle. Sustainability is built in.

What did I learn?

Knowledge // concepts

  • Logistics is a subset of supply chain management — specifically the moving and storing.
  • Supply chains carry four flows: goods downstream, cash and returns upstream, information both ways.
  • Internal vs external logistics, and chain types: product, information, service, reverse, circular.

Skills // abilities

  • Systems thinking — mapping a whole network instead of one isolated shipment.
  • Translating a static textbook diagram into an interactive model with SVG and JavaScript.
  • Spotting where each later concept (cost, mode, warehouse) plugs into the chain.

Reflection // real world

  • Surprising: money and returns flow backwards — the chain isn't a one-way street.
  • Difficult: deciding which flows to show without the diagram becoming noise.
  • Applied: the same map explains an online order, a car factory, or a circular recycling loop.
