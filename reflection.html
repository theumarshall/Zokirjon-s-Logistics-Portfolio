Five concepts delivered. Here's what stood out, what fought back, what mattered most — and how the whole subject looked different by the end.

01

The three standouts

Across the five builds, three earned a special mention — one for curiosity, one for difficulty, one for lasting usefulness.

Most interesting 03

Modes of Transportation

This one was the most fun to think about because there's no single right answer. The same shipment can demand opposite modes depending on one variable — drop urgency and air gives way to ocean; change the product to liquid and a pipeline suddenly wins. Building a scoring engine forced me to make those trade-offs explicit instead of hand-wavy, and watching the cost-versus-speed map react was genuinely satisfying.

Revisit project 03 → Most challenging 02

The Cost of Logistics

The freight cost calculator was the hardest to get right. The trap was the fuel surcharge: fuel is a cost the carrier pays, yet the surcharge is revenue that offsets it — model it carelessly and you double-count. Add deadhead miles and idle waiting time, and a lane that looks profitable quietly turns into a loss. Getting the model honest — and instructive — took the most iterations of any project.

Revisit project 02 → Most valuable 01

Role of Logistics in Supply Chains

The flow explorer turned out to be the most valuable, because it's the lens everything else hangs on. Once I could see the four flows and where internal versus external logistics happen, every later topic had a home: cost lives on the arrows, modes connect the tiers, warehouses sit between them, and 3PLs run the whole thing. It's the map I kept coming back to.

02

How my understanding changed

The clearest way to show what I learned is to compare where I started with where I finished.

ORIGIN Before

  • Logistics meant "shipping" — trucks and boxes moving from A to B.
  • I assumed there was usually one obvious, best way to move something.
  • Cost felt like a single sticker price, not something you build up piece by piece.
  • A warehouse was simply a place to keep things.
  • "3PL" and "4PL" were interchangeable buzzwords.
➔

DELIVERED After

  • Logistics is a networked system of flows — goods, information, money and returns.
  • Every choice is a trade-off: speed vs cost, flexibility vs capacity, control vs convenience.
  • Cost is assembled from miles, fuel, labour, idle time and fixed overhead — and thin margins punish mistakes.
  • A warehouse is a decision: store goods, or flow them straight through.
  • The PL ladder marks real shifts in who owns assets and who designs the chain.
The big shift: I stopped seeing logistics as "moving stuff" and started seeing it as a series of costed decisions across a connected network. That reframing — from activity to system — is the single most important thing I took from the course.
