Warehousing & Cross-Docking

A warehouse doesn't have to store anything. Sometimes the smartest move is to flow goods straight through — receive, sort, reship the same day.

1

Project overview

Warehousing is the "storing" half of logistics — but Chapter 5 makes a sharp distinction. Traditional storage receives goods, puts them away, holds them for days, then picks and ships. Cross-docking skips the storage entirely: the dock becomes a staging area where inbound shipments transition straight to outbound trucks. Fewer touches, no dwell time — but it only works when demand is predictable.

  • What inspired it: the chapter's contrast between distribution-centre storage and cross-docking, where "there is no actual storage involved."
  • The concept: every time a pallet is handled or sits in storage, it costs money. Cross-docking removes whole steps — but adds a coordination requirement.
  • Why it matters: storage space and handling labour are two of the largest warehouse costs. Knowing when to flow versus store is a real budget decision.

What did I learn?

Knowledge // concepts

  • The warehouse types — consolidation, cross-dock, break-bulk, hub-and-spoke, DC.
  • That cross-docking involves no actual storage — the dock is a staging area.
  • Storage cost has two halves: handling touches and dwell time.

Skills // abilities

  • Comparative cost modelling of two operating strategies.
  • Building a break-even the user can move by changing assumptions.
  • Designing a stacked chart that shows why one option is cheaper.

Reflection // real world

  • Surprising: a warehouse that stores nothing can be the cheapest option.
  • Difficult: capturing that cross-docking trades storage cost for coordination cost.
  • Applied: grocery and retail chains cross-dock fast-moving stock for exactly these savings.
